## The Lilong Where I Grew Up

Most of Hongkou's lane houses are gone now. But if I close my eyes, I can still smell them — the coal smoke at dawn, the watermelon chilled in well water on summer afternoons, the neighbor's sun-dried plums. The lane was five minutes end to end, but those five minutes held an entire world.

We lived on the second floor. The wooden stairs groaned. Our window faced the courtyard, where laundry sagged under every color. At dusk, Suzhou pingtan drifted from radios. Grown-ups pulled bamboo chairs into the lane to catch the evening breeze. Children ran wild. We weren't rich, but neighbors were family. Someone made wontons, a bowl went next door. A child was unattended, a grandmother would appear.

There was a "tiger stove" at the lane entrance — a shop selling boiled water, one fen per flask. Every evening I'd go with our thermos. Waiting in line, I'd hear all the news: the Zhang boy got into university, the Li family had a new baby, Old Wang's cat ran away again. That little shop was the CNN of our lane.

Young people today live in high-rises, behind doors that never open. I think — it's comfortable, yes, but something was lost with the lanes. The feeling of pushing open your door and walking straight into the warmth of life. The knowledge that if something happened, a whole street would come to help. Elevators and central air don't replace that.

## Letters Across the Straits

In 1962, I received a letter forwarded through Hong Kong. The envelope was crumpled, bearing three stamps. It was from my best friend in middle school — Lin Shuzhen. She had gone to Taiwan with her family in '49. We'd had no contact since.

I read that letter more times than I can count. She had become a teacher in Taipei, teaching Chinese literature. The winters were cold too, she wrote, but not as damp as Shanghai. She missed me terribly. She wanted to eat xiaolongbao with me at the City God Temple again. The last line read: "Xiulan, do you remember the wish we made under the ginkgo tree at No.1 School?"

I remembered. We promised to become teachers together. We both did — just on opposite sides of a strait.

For over thirty years, our letters crossed the sea in fits and starts. We never discussed politics — only books, flowers, students, children. Every letter took months. Sometimes they arrived nearly destroyed. But those thin sheets were our hands reaching toward each other across the distance.

In the 1990s, she finally returned to Shanghai. We met at the Bund — two old women, holding each other, weeping. Her hair white, teeth missing — but when she smiled, she was sixteen again. That day I took her for xiaolongbao, just as we'd promised forty years before.

I told this story to my granddaughter. She said, "Grandma, you all had it so hard." I said: hardship is ordinary. What matters is holding onto the people and things that are precious. That's the meaning of a life.

## Teaching Three Generations

The most wonderful thing in my teaching career — I taught three generations of one family.

In the 1970s, I taught a boy named Zhou Jianguo. Clever but restless, he could never sit still. I didn't scold him — I made him the class Chinese monitor. The responsibility transformed him. Trust given became trust earned. He went on to university and became an engineer.

In the 1990s, his daughter Zhou Xiaoyan was assigned to my class. When I saw her name and address on the roster, I asked: "Is your father Zhou Jianguo?" The girl's eyes went wide — nodding furiously. I told her: your father sat third row, by the window.

Years later, Zhou Xiaoyan brought her son to visit me. She said she wanted him to call me Teacher Chen too. I said: don't call me Teacher Chen. Call me Grandma.

If you teach long enough, you collect a few stories like this. They're what made those forty years at the podium worth it. Teaching isn't writing on a blackboard. It's putting something into another person's heart — and watching it bloom, years later, into flowers you never expected.
