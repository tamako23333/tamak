<h2>Father's Parker Fountain Pen</h2>
<p>A Parker fountain pen with a 14k gold nib — my father's legacy. He kept the textile mill's accounts with it for decades, every entry precise and upright. "Bookkeeping is like character," he used to say. "Every stroke must answer to your conscience."</p>
<p>The pen no longer writes. The black lacquer on the barrel has worn through, the brass showing beneath. But every time I look at it, I see my father bent over his ledger — the lamp low, the abacus clicking, the pen scratching steadily. If the old man knew I was still writing — with a ballpoint now, admittedly — still putting words to paper, he'd probably nod and smile.</p>
<p>Sometimes I think: the most valuable inheritance isn't a house or savings. It's a pen like this — holding the warmth of a father's hand, and his quiet insistence on doing things right.</p>

<h2>The Famille Rose Tea Set</h2>
<p>A famille rose tea set — one pot, four cups — a wedding gift from Lao Chen's mother. Supposedly a Jingdezhen antique. White porcelain painted with peonies — red, pink, purple — blooming exuberantly on the creamy glaze. Sixty years later, one cup has broken, and the knob on the teapot lid is chipped. But we've kept what remains.</p>
<p>Every New Year, I brew a pot of Longjing in this teapot. After Lao Chen passed, I sit with the tea alone. It still feels like he's in the next chair — quiet, just being there. The tea goes cold now. Nobody comes to warm it anymore.</p>
<p>This tea set witnessed the beginning of a family. It's no longer complete, but it's still here. Some things are treasures precisely because they're a little broken.</p>

<h2>The Handwritten Poetry Notebooks</h2>
<p>The day I retired, Lao Chen gave me a notebook. Since then, one after another, I've filled over a dozen — some with brush pen, some with ballpoint. Inside are poems (mostly doggerel); life sketches recording the osmanthus blooming in the courtyard, the neighbor's cat having kittens, my granddaughter's first word; letters to Lao Chen and the children (some sent, some not); and favorite quotes I've copied — Lu Xun, Eileen Chang, Wang Zengqi.</p>
<p>The handwriting gets shakier as the pages turn, because my hands don't obey me like they used to. The first notebook is neat and firm; by the tenth, the lines have begun to waver. That's the trace of aging. But I'm not afraid of it — what's inside these notebooks matters more than youth ever did.</p>
<p>My granddaughter says they should be made into a proper book. I tell her: no rush. Let me fill a few more first. When you reach your eighties, the most precious things aren't grand revelations — they're the quiet joys and honest sorrows of each ordinary day, faithfully recorded.</p>
