## The Lilong Where I Grew Up

Most of Hongkou's lane houses are gone now. But if I close my eyes, I can still smell them — the coal smoke at dawn, the watermelon chilled in well water on summer afternoons, the neighbor's sun-dried plums. The lane wasn't long — five minutes from end to end — but those five minutes held an entire world.

We lived on the second floor. The wooden stairs groaned with every step. Our window faced the courtyard, where laundry poles sagged under clothes of every color. At dusk, the sound of Suzhou pingtan drifted from radios. Grown-ups pulled bamboo chairs into the lane entrance to catch the evening breeze. Children ran wild. We weren't rich, but neighbors were like family. If someone made wontons, a bowl always went next door. If a child was left unattended, some grandmother would always look after them.

Young people today live in high-rises, behind doors that never open to strangers. I sometimes think — it's comfortable, yes, but something was lost with the lanes. Something that can't be rebuilt.

## Letters Across the Straits

In 1962, I received a letter forwarded through Hong Kong. The envelope was crumpled beyond recognition, bearing three stamps. It was from my best friend in middle school — Lin Shuzhen. She had gone to Taiwan with her family in '49. We'd had no contact since.

I read that letter more times than I can count. She had become a teacher in Taipei, teaching Chinese literature. The winters in Taipei were cold too, she wrote, but not as damp as Shanghai. She missed me terribly. She wanted to go eat xiaolongbao with me at the City God Temple again, like we used to.

For over thirty years, our letters crossed the strait in fits and starts. We never discussed politics — only books, flowers, students, children. In the 1990s, she finally returned to Shanghai. We met at the Bund. Two old women, holding each other, weeping. Her hair had gone white. She'd lost a few teeth. But when she smiled, she was still sixteen.

I told this story to my granddaughter. She said, "Grandma, you all had it so hard." I said: hardship is ordinary. What matters is holding onto the people and things that are precious. That's the meaning of a life.

![Old letters and photographs](https://picsum.photos/seed/old-letters/1200/600)

## Teaching Three Generations

The strangest and most wonderful thing in my teaching career — I taught three generations of one family.

In the 1970s, I taught a boy named Zhou Jianguo. He was clever but restless. I made him the class Chinese monitor, and the responsibility transformed him. He went on to university and became an engineer. In the 1990s, his daughter Zhou Xiaoyan was assigned to my class. When I saw her name and address on the roster, I asked: "Is your father Zhou Jianguo?" The girl's eyes went wide. Years later, around 2015, Zhou Xiaoyan brought her son to visit me. She said she wanted her son to call me Teacher Chen too.

I told her: don't call me Teacher Chen. Call me Grandma.

If you teach long enough, you collect a few stories like this. They're what made those forty years at the podium worth it.

## Father's Fountain Pen

A Parker fountain pen, with a 14k gold nib — my father's legacy. He kept the mill's accounts with it for decades, every entry precise and upright. "Bookkeeping is like character," he used to say. "Every stroke must answer to your conscience."

The pen no longer writes. The black lacquer on the barrel has worn through in places, the brass showing beneath. But every time I look at it, I see my father bent over his ledger. If the old man knew I was still writing — with a ballpoint now, admittedly — still putting words to paper, he'd probably nod and smile.

![Vintage Parker fountain pen](https://picsum.photos/seed/fountain-pen-vintage/800/600)

## The Porcelain Tea Set

A famille rose tea set — one pot, four cups — a wedding gift from Lao Chen's mother. Supposedly a Jingdezhen antique, the peonies painted on it exquisitely. Sixty years later, one cup has broken, and the knob on the teapot lid is chipped. But we've kept what remains.

Every New Year, I brew a pot of Longjing in this teapot. After Lao Chen passed, I sit with the tea alone. It still feels like he's in the next chair.

![Famille rose porcelain tea set](https://picsum.photos/seed/chinese-tea-set/800/600)

## The Handwritten Poetry Notebooks

Since 1996, I've filled over a dozen notebooks — some with brush pen, some with ballpoint. Inside are poems (mostly doggerel), life sketches, letters to Lao Chen and the children, favorite quotes I've copied down. The handwriting gets shakier as the pages turn, because my hands don't obey me like they used to. But opening any one of these notebooks is like stepping into another era.

My granddaughter says they should be made into a proper book. I tell her: no rush. Let me fill a few more first.

![Handwritten poetry notebooks](https://picsum.photos/seed/handwritten-notebook/800/600)

Truth be told, this website is the book I always wanted. Better than a book, really — more people can see it, it can hold photographs, and I can update it anytime. If my father were still here, I think he'd marvel at what the world has become.
